Meet Wine Notes

Wine can be confusing, Wine Notes is here to fix that. Wine Notes helps users understand a traditionally esoteric beverage by relating it back to something we can all get behind: food. Users discover wine they’ll love by the brand’s flavor-focused highlights which guide the entire design.

Overview

THE CHALLENGE

What’s an
appellation,
anyways?

Varietal, vintage, appellation, region—traditional wine e-commerce websites structure their information architecture and content by classifying buckets that mean little in terms of flavor to someone who is not well acquainted to wine.

How do we redesign the wine shopping experience to make flavor understandable to an every day (or not so often!) consumer?

THE SOLUTION

Every aspect of Wine Notes was designed to relate wine back to the smell and flavors found in nature, a produce aisle, or out in the garden. Flavor rules the hierarchy. Product pages boast prominent flavor profile images along with called out highlights like pairings and sweetness. Product filtering options are taste-centric, allowing users to quickly find and match wines based on flavor preferences.

To further inform buying decisions Wine Notes implements a rating and review system which allows customers the option to leave written reviews and rate how closely the product descriptions actually align with the true wine flavor.

COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS

The internet has an array of options for buying wine. I set out to understand if any of these companies were addressing what my research showed to be the most pressing problem— understanding what any given bottle of wine tastes like. I ran a competitive analysis of three popular e-commerce sites: Fresh Direct, Verve Wine, and Winc.

Fresh Direct is a local NYC food and beverage delivery company. Right from the beginning, the website was not an inviting place to explore and learn about wine. There were no reviews or high-level information on flavor. Poor product photos soured the experience further. However, customers benefit from the one-stop shopping experience since they can buy groceries too. Convenience at its finest.

Verve Wine was founded by Master Sommelier Dustin Wilson. For all its information on wine, there wasn’t an easy to understand product flavor profile. This leaves the assumption that users already know what a riesling or merlot tastes like from Rhone. Once again there were no customer reviews. Verve Wine’s elegant design with intuitive navigation, tagging system, and recommendations was an inspiration for Wine Notes..

The final e-commerce site I explored was Winc, a wine club. Winc doesn’t offer written reviews and users have to commit to their wine club subscription. But if you’re into trying new wines, a wine club could be the way to go! Winc’s product page offered a fantastic flavor profile–– by far it’s biggest strength.

These competitors inspired features for Wine Notes while also shining a light on untapped opportunities—pportunities I knew users wanted from my initial research.

TESTING LOFI
WIREFRAMES

Refine giving
a review

I assumed that keeping the ability to leave a review on the product page would be a “lighter” and easier experience than needing to creating an account. I was wrong.

I found that 80% of test takers wanted to log into an account to leave a review. Even more cumbersome—some test-takers backtracked through every step they had previously taken to checkout in order to locate the wine they wanted to review. I needed to pivot from my original design.

I went back and sketched out a new onboarding experience and account page. From here users could directly view previous orders and leave reviews. Lesson learned: people leave reviews from their account page, not a product page.

TESTING LOFI
WIREFRAMES

Refine
checking out

Friction also popped up as test takers moved through checkout. I thought I was being clever by including 3 screens in one for checkout. As people moved through inputting their information, next buttons were supposed to appear indicating it was time to move to the next section. This was lost on people. Users tried jumping ahead to shipping before completing billing. To ease the cognitive load I reduced the transparency on all inactive fields. I also added an inactive “next” button from the very start to help show when it was time to move on.

Branding

The Branding of scent

My original high fidelity mockups laid the groundwork for Wine Note’s branding. By visually exploring the product I become intimately familiar with the hues and color palette associated with it. The playfulness and color saturation of fruit would ultimately influence the brand identity.

Brand Personality & Values:
bright, earth, fresh, friendly
educational, sensory, simplicity

The name

The chosen name acts as a double entendre. First, and most obvious, it conveys what the product provides: notes and information on wine. Second, the flavor and scent we experience while drinking wine is also called a “note”. Wine notes are what the shop is committed to exploring with customers.

The logo

Getting to the final logo was a messy affair. I sketched dozens of designs, explored 5 different directions, returned to mind mapping multiple times, and ultimately ran two different logo preference tests before choosing my final variation.

The logo

A winner in
the chaos!

The final logo mark and typeface capture the shape of a wine bottle label. Two bright overlapping semi-ovals forms both a “W” and three wine glasses. The colors represent the different flavors found in any given glass. The Montserrat typeface was modified and softened for the logo, with sharp corners rounded off to create a more organic and soft feel.

The colors

The brand needed to convey a sense of taste and smell while also being welcoming. I chose a color palette that complimented wine and fruit, but let the product shine. A calm green spoke to the grape’s origin while conveying a peaceful, inviting, and educational place––great for those intimidated by the space. A bright purple encouraged action and brought energy to the design.

The Typography

Wine Notes uses only one typeface, Montserrat. Montserrat was inspired by the old posters and signs in the traditional Montserrat neighborhood of Buenos Aires. According to its designer, “the letters that inspired this project have work, dedication, care, color, contrast, light and life, day and night!”. Within the Wine Notes’ brand, Montserrat creates a modern, welcoming, and friendly feel.

Visual Design & Testing

Preference
testing: filters

An important component of any e-commerce site is the ability to sort and filter products. I wanted to better understand if users preferred text alone or text with icons to help them navigate the product menu. Using usabilityhub.com I conducted a preference test for two different designs. The question was simple: Which menu is easier to navigate? The one with fruit icons or the one without?

I received 18 responses for my test, 56% of which preferred the menu with icons. One test taker summarized its success by saying, “There is a lot of information to sift through and the icons make that section quicker to read.”

Although the test was not statistically significant I decided to move forward with a navigation system that incorporated icons and text.

FINAL PRODUCT

What's In Store

Reflections:

To further Wine Notes’ mission of demystifying fermented juice, future phases should focus on sharing educational materials and a wine preference test.

The creation of Wine Notes has sparked my imagination for what could come next. Initial user research revealed that 78.6% of survey-takers would opt to take a wine preference test online. Additionally, 92.9% of respondents would read more about the region, grape, or winery.

A test to understand user’s flavor preferences would make the website even stronger––and some competitors are already doing this. As always, further research should be conducted to discover what else would help users feel more confident in the choices they make when buying wine.

Continue to question the norm.

As we continue to design e-commerce experiences, question well-established information architecture and content––because the user on the other side of the screen might just be wondering, “what’s an appellation anyways?”
