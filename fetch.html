Meet Fetch

In the United States, sixty-eight percent of U.S. households, or about 85 million families, own a pet. Animals are now an integral part of our family structure, however, technology for pets hasn't caught up. Enter, Fetch.

This IOS app is on a mission to make managing pet data and care, simple. Owners can access medical records, share care instructions, set reminders, and track pet fitness. Never will a dog be lost again, a cat overweight or a rabies vaccination missed (OK, your cat may still be overweight, but you get the gist).

Understanding Pet Owners

Pain points &
solutions

I set out to understand the mentality of pet owners and their everyday pain points. To narrow down priority features I surveyed over 30 pet owners.
Check out the user survey and survey takeaways

Four main takeaways emerged from the results:

  1. Trust in thy neighbor. Owners wanted to feel assured that caretakers were systematically following instructions and overseeing pets with attention to detail.

  2. Records = hot mess. Vet records existed in paper files scattered around the house, online at the vet's office, or lost forever somewhere in between. Owners needed an organized system where looking up a record was as easy as a walk in the park.

  3. Teamwork makes the dream work. Having a way to remind themselves and family members about appointments, expired vaccinations, and daily walks were key.

  4. Fitness isn't a trend. A pet needs exercise and an owner needs data. By using a smart collar (pet wearable), stats and mapping allow users to stay on top of daily pet fitness levels.

There are numerous applications to help us care for pets and yet each one is pigeon-holed. There are applications for managing medical records (VitusVet), and others for managing pet sitters (Rover), and still more for smart collars (Whistle 3). Information Architecture

The (user) stories we told

With the pain points uncovered and an MVP narrowed down, I developed four user stories and flows. Check out all user stories and all user flows.

  1. The ability to create a pet profile that contains care instructions and can be easily shared with veterinarians, pet care services, and friends and family.

  2. A medical record system that allows users to request records from their veterinarians, manually input data, or add their own notes.

  3. Ability to track pet's activity each day and instantly locate them through a smart device.

  4. A calendar system for coordinating appointments, care responsibilities, and pet-related reminders

Branding

Getting all
dressed up: the
brand

Fetch lives at the intersection of friendly, inviting (the warm and fuzzies you get with your pet), and professional. Although the primary users are pet owners, the company also markets itself to business owners. The color palette reflects this juxtaposition–a serious blue with a playful and invigorating yellow and green. The green also served as a nod to where all animals come from, nature.

The font choice reflects the brand’s modern values. Ubuntu is used in the logotype and as headers. It is a humanist style typeface commonly known for being modern, clean, and technical.

After extensive mind mapping, the name “Fetch” was selected. Fetch was derived from the idea of caretaking. One of the most basic aspects of owning a pet is giving it amble exercise, attention, and play. Although the game of fetch is often associated with dogs, it is also a fun game for cats. From a technological standpoint, the name captures the essence of the app–fetching information. When the concepts of play, care, and data are tied together, the name “Fetch” accurately represents the brand positioning.

Visual Design & Testing

The vision
sketches &
wireframes

My goal was to design solutions for the four main pain points uncovered in the initial user surveys. I looked to the top apps on the market for inspiration––Apple’s health app, Rover’s pet profile, Fitbit’s analytics, and the seamlessness of Instagram’s multiple account menus.

A significant amount of information goes into explaining a pet's story (personality, needs, likes/dislikes). The first challenge was designing an onboarding process that collected a large volume of information while also feeling engaging and fast.

From the pet profile, users can view and edit care instructions, set up their smart collar, and share profile information with contacts.

Within the health hub, big buttons allow pet owners to quickly filter by record type. A tagging system and search bar were also integrated to improve speed in locating a specific record. Records can be directly requested from veterinarians or manually added by a user.

The fitness page aims for simplicity through color and image. Owners see active minutes and can easily toggle back in time for a full history of pet activity.

Users receive notifications for upcoming appointments and various other reminders from the calendar section. Reminders can easily be scheduled and shared both internally on the app and with external calendars. Recurring events were added as a feature based on user feedback.

Testing it all out

To discover design pain points, I utilized both in-person interviews and remote testing. Remote tests provided analytics and heatmaps to help us better understand user behavior, while in-person interviews filled us in with qualitative feedback. Participants completed the following 3 task scenarios.

  1. Add a pet profile
  2. Add a medical record
  3. Schedule a reminder
Add a pet profile

The pet onboarding process had the highest success rate of the 3 tasks (90%). That didn’t mean it was without flaws. I thought I had reduced the steps for creating a pet profile to the bare minimum, but users disagreed. They wanted fewer steps and less copy.

To cut down on the 8 onboarding screens I combined similar information together. Instead of one screen to select a pet type and another to select a gender, I grouped both questions together. This reduced clicks, lessened user fatigue, and shortened the progress bar. Ultimately onboarding went from 8 steps to 6. The pre-onboarding screen was reduced from 26 words to just 7. Further testing found that users were satisfied with the improved flow and had a close to 100% success rate.

Add a medical record

Maze results showed a 40% give up/bounce rate for adding a medical record. This was due in part to the way the prototype’s hot spots were set up but also uploading options. Users had three options: “request records,” “take a photo,” or “manually add”. What’s the difference between “take a photo” and “manually add”? Good question!

I realized there was no reason to break these into 2 options. Users only required two paths: request a record or add one themselves. I combined the two options and then updated the copy so instructions were crystal clear: “request record from veterinarian” or “manually enter record”.

Schedule a reminder

This task scenario is where things really broke down.

The first hurdle for users was the concept of notifications vs reminders. I was asking them to schedule a reminder, something that feels time-bound while labeling the section as “notifications”. Although I equated notifications to reminders, users did not. One tester said, “It’s a little confusing because it seems that the list [of notifications] is actually a list of reminders that need to be completed in the future but haven’t been completed yet. And to me, notifications are ‘real time’ things that need to be done now or should have been done already.”

I renamed this section to “reminders” and then got to work on the list of additional features that were suggested during testing. I added in the ability to make recurring reminders, clarified who would be receiving the reminder, along with adding a location and the ability to sync with Google Calendar.

Additionally, I cleaned up styling across the app to build repetition and thus ease of use. This allowed users to learn that the “save” function will always be in the top right corner (which wasn’t clear to users before) and that all submissions are made through a button at the bottom of the screen.

Preference test: pet
profile

The pet profile is one of the most central elements in Fetch. It houses a large amount of information about a pet while simultaneously aiming to quickly deliver high-level information. The original design solution relied on a scrolling screen with optional chevrons to go deeper and colored icons to quickly call attention to information. I created two alternative solutions which chunked information into 3 categories. I hypothesized that users would feel less overwhelmed with information broken up. I was also curious if the pet profile picture size and shape would effect preference as well. I was certain that version 2 would be preferred given that it seemed quicker to navigate to specific information (here’s version 3). I was wrong. 86% of respondents preferred the scrolling screen.

“The colored icons in the extended profile gives me a lot of info very easily.”

Half of the respondents who preferred the first design mentioned that they chose it because of the colored icon list. The icons had the ability to quickly call attention to key information about a pet, reducing the cognitive load of finding information. Additionally, users did prefer seeing all the information laid out on one screen without needing to click on additional filters to learn more. A bigger pet profile picture was also preferred.

Takeaways: make it visual with icons, call attention to information with color, show off the pups (a bigger pet profile picture is better)

Preference test:
medical records

A centralized hub for digital medical records is another important component within Fetch. I wanted to ensure that users could quickly any medical record they may be searching for. The app allows for tagging, categories, and filters. But I wanted to take it a step further by using color or icons to help users scan for information more quickly. My preference test aimed to determine if color or icons more quickly help a user identify a record type. Icons won.

“The icons tell me more about what the record is about–with the color-coding, I have to page back to reference what the colors mean. The icons are more self-explanatory!”

Takeaways: by making it visual through the use of icons, users can more quickly determine a record type.

Reflections

Summary

My guiding principle in Fetch was the well-known quote by Antoine de Saint-Exupery: "A designer knows (s)he has achieved perfection not when there is nothing left to add, but when there is nothing left to take away." I aimed to keep the design intuitive and clean while delivering the many components necessary in creating a holistic approach to pet care.

WHAT WORKED

  1. The concept. Users rejoiced in the idea of one platform housing all their pet's data and care information. The overall product appealed to almost every pet owner I interviewed.

  2. Pet onboarding process was praised throughout user testing; it transformed a normally bland and laborious experience into one of enjoyment.

  3. Oversized pet photo and extended pet profile (with its high-level callouts through use of iconography and color) helped users meet a pet at a glance.

WHAT WORKED

  1. The project ran 3 weeks past my original delivery date. Frequent user testing and feedback was a priority but the time required to do this well was underestimated. I learned the necessity of building in extra cushion time to allow for additional user testing.

  2. Sticking to the user stories wasn't always easy with so many ideas flowing in from user testing. I was tempted to try out solutions and features that were not a part of the MVP. I revisited original objectives frequently to ensure I stayed on track with the timeline and deliverables.

LOOKING TO THE
FUTURE

Fetch aims to be the leading pet management system, and I believe to be a leader means to be a collaborator. I plan on future build-outs integrating with other leading software (Fitbit, Google Suite, and leading veterinarian tech platforms). The next phase has two goals:

  1. Build out a working prototype for small business owners who use Fetch to work with pet owners.

  2. Make acquiring medical records even more seamless by applying TrippIt-like technology so records can immediately be added from email to the health hub.

It was an absolute pleasure designing Fetch from the ground up and ensuring the design process shaped each decision I made in developing the product. Here’s to simplifying pet management, so we can get back to the important stuff, spending time with our furry friends.
